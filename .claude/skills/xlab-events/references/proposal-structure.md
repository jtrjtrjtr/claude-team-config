# Proposal Structure — Framework & Best Practices

## Purpose

This document defines how XLAB proposals are structured. It combines patterns observed across real XLAB proposals (Česká spořitelna, Visa, MHAP, Bachledka, Pilsner Urquell) with international event industry best practices.

## Universal Principles

### The Proposal Paradox
A proposal must simultaneously prove two contradictory things:
1. "We understood exactly what you need" (empathy, listening, precision)
2. "We thought of things you didn't" (vision, expertise, added value)

The balance between these two defines a great proposal.

### The 3-Second Rule
Every page must pass the scan test — a reader glancing for 3 seconds should know: what section they're in, what the key message is, and where to look next. This means clear headers, visual hierarchy, and no wall-of-text pages.

### Evidence Over Adjectives
"Award-winning team" → "Team of 12 with 200+ completed events including [specific reference]"
"Cutting-edge technology" → "4K laser projection, 15,000 lm per unit, automated calibration"
"Immersive experience" → "360° dome projection with 4.1 surround audio and real-time interactive layer"

## Proposal Architecture

### Module System
XLAB proposals are built from modules. Not every proposal needs every module. Select based on event type and brief requirements.

#### A. Opening Block (always present)

**A1. Cover**
- Project name, client name, date, "Prepared by XLAB" + partner if applicable
- Generated from xlab-proposal skill (Layout 1)

**A2. About XLAB / Team** (1–2 slides)
- Not generic company bio — tailored to the brief
- Highlight relevant capabilities, team members by name with roles
- Include specific references that match the brief's context
- If partnering: introduce partner and explain collaboration model

**A3. Brief Understanding** (1 slide)
- Prove comprehension: restate the challenge in XLAB's own words
- Identify the core opportunity or tension the event must resolve
- This is NOT a brief summary; it's an interpretation that shows insight

#### B. Strategic Block (for enhanced and full creative proposals)

**B1. Strategic Context** (1 slide)
- Client's brand position, audience dynamics, market context
- The "why" behind the event — what success looks like
- Communication framework or campaign platform alignment

**B2. Audience Insight** (1 slide, when relevant)
- Who are the attendees beyond demographics?
- What do they expect? What would surprise them?
- Behavioral considerations: attention span, mobility, digital habits

#### C. Creative Block (the core — scale varies by type)

**C1. Creative Concept** (1–3 slides)
- The throughline idea — one sentence, one visual, one metaphor
- How it manifests across the entire event
- Why it's right for THIS client, THIS audience, THIS moment

**C2. Attendee Journey** (1–3 slides)
- Step-by-step from arrival to departure
- For experiential: zone-by-zone with transitions
- Include emotional arc: what the attendee feels at each stage
- Spatial diagram or flow illustration

**C3. Design & Visual World** (1–3 slides)
- Stage design, spatial layout, key visual moments
- Moodboard or visual direction
- Material palette, lighting concept, color world

**C4. Program / Run-of-Show** (1–2 slides)
- Timeline with key moments
- For conferences: agenda structure
- For shows: cue-by-cue or act structure
- For installations: operating cycle

**C5. Technology & Production** (1–2 slides)
- What technology is used, why, and how
- Not a spec sheet — a narrative of how tech enables the experience
- Key specs in supporting table format
- Redundancy and reliability approach

**C6. Content Production** (1 slide, when applicable)
- What content is produced (video, animation, interactive, generative)
- Production methodology and timeline
- Software / pipeline overview
- Deliverable format

#### D. Proof Block (always present)

**D1. References** (1–2 slides)
- 3–5 relevant past projects with visuals
- Match reference type to brief type
- Brief description + key metric if available

**D2. Team** (1 slide)
- Named individuals with photo, role, relevant experience
- Show depth: not just creative lead but production, technical, onsite

#### E. Commercial Block

**E1. Budget Overview** (1 slide in proposal)
- Summary level in presentation
- Detailed kalkulace as separate Excel attachment
- Present variants if applicable (standard / premium / phased)

**E2. Timeline** (1 slide)
- Key milestones from agreement to delivery
- Highlight critical path items
- Show client dependencies (approvals, venue access, content delivery)

#### F. Closing Block

**F1. Next Steps** (1 slide)
- Clear call to action
- What XLAB needs to proceed
- Contact information

**F2. Closing** (from template)
- Generated from xlab-proposal skill (Layout 15)

## Type-Specific Configurations

### Corporate Conference
Modules: A1, A2, A3, B1, C2, C3, C4, C5, D1, D2, E1, E2, F1, F2
Focus weight: 40% execution detail (C2–C5), 30% proof (D), 20% understanding (A3+B1), 10% commercial

### Brand Activation
Modules: A1, A2, A3, C1, C2, C3, C5, C6, D1, E1, E2, F1, F2
Focus weight: 50% creative (C1–C3), 20% production (C5–C6), 20% proof, 10% commercial

### Experiential Launch
Modules: A1, A2, A3, B1, B2, C1, C2, C3, C4, C5, C6, D1, D2, E1, E2, F1, F2
Focus weight: 40% creative + journey (C1–C3), 25% production (C4–C6), 20% strategic (B), 15% proof + commercial

### Immersive Installation
Modules: A1, A2, A3, C1, C2, C3, C5, C6, D1, E1, E2, F1, F2
Plus separate: Technical Architecture Document + Content Production Plan
Focus weight: 35% creative concept, 35% technical, 20% content production, 10% commercial

### Agency RFP
Modules: A1, A2, D1, D2, plus custom capability responses and rate card
Focus weight: 40% capabilities + case studies, 30% team, 30% pricing

## Writing the Proposal — Process

1. **Start with the Brief Card** (from intake). Confirm all facts.
2. **Choose modules** based on event type and brief requirements.
3. **Write C1 first** (creative concept). Everything else flows from the throughline.
4. **Build C2** (journey) immediately after — the concept must be grounded in experience.
5. **Draft remaining C modules** to prove the concept works in practice.
6. **Write A3 and B block** — reframe the brief through the lens of the proposed concept.
7. **Assemble D block** — select references that validate the approach.
8. **Budget and timeline** come last — they quantify decisions already made.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **The Feature List Proposal**: listing capabilities without connecting them to the brief
- **The Generic Insert**: reusing slides that don't reference the specific client or event
- **The Technology-First Pitch**: leading with projector specs before establishing the experience
- **The Missing Journey**: describing what exists in the space but not what happens to the person in it
- **The Vague Budget**: "pricing upon request" instead of at least a range or variant structure
- **The Confident Promise**: committing to specific outcomes without caveats or dependencies
- **The Endless Proposal**: 80+ slides when 20 would make the case stronger

## Formatting Standards

- Proposals use English for international / corporate clients
- Use XLAB branded template via xlab-proposal skill
- Black theme (Layouts 1–15) for standard proposals
- White theme (Layouts 16–30) for extended/premium proposals
- Maximum 25 slides for standard proposal; 40 for extended
- Every slide has a clear title and one core message
- Visuals > text wherever possible
