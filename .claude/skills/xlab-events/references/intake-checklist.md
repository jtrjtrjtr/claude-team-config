# Intake Checklist — Brief Deconstruction Framework

## Purpose

This framework ensures no incoming brief is processed on autopilot. Every brief — from a 2-line email to a 50-page RFP — goes through the same analytical filter before any creative or commercial work begins.

## 1. Raw Extraction

Pull every stated fact from the brief into a flat list. Do not interpret; just capture.

### Client & Context
- Client name, industry, market position
- Contact person(s) and their role (marketing, procurement, C-suite, agency intermediary)
- Relationship status: new client / returning / through partner / competitive pitch
- Confidentiality requirements

### Event Fundamentals
- Event name (official or working title)
- Date(s) and duration
- Location / venue (confirmed or candidate list)
- Number of attendees (confirmed, expected, capacity)
- Attendee profile: employees, customers, partners, VIP, general public, mixed
- Event language(s)

### Scope & Deliverables
- What is being asked for? (full event production, AV only, concept only, installation, etc.)
- Listed deliverables
- Format: physical / hybrid / virtual / installation
- Is this a one-off or recurring?

### Commercial
- Stated budget (total and/or per-component)
- Budget currency
- Who controls the budget? (client direct, agency, procurement)
- Payment terms if specified
- Travel/accommodation considerations

### Process
- Submission deadline
- Presentation round expected?
- Evaluation criteria (if stated)
- Decision timeline
- Number of competing agencies

### Creative
- Is there a stated theme, message, or campaign platform?
- Visual guidelines / brand book provided?
- How open is the creative brief? (tight → open scale)
- Reference materials, moodboards, or past event documentation?

## 2. Gap Analysis

For each category above, explicitly identify what is NOT provided. These gaps become clarification questions (if timeline permits) or assumptions to state in the proposal.

### Critical Gaps (block progress if unknown)
- Budget: Without at least a range, scope cannot be defined
- Audience size: Affects every technical and logistical decision
- Date: Drives feasibility and team availability
- Creative scope: Determines proposal effort and approach

### Important Gaps (address in proposal)
- Venue specifics (dimensions, technical infrastructure, access times)
- Detailed agenda or run-of-show
- Technical rider or existing AV inventory
- Sustainability / accessibility requirements
- Post-event deliverables (video, report, content)

### Nice-to-Know Gaps (note for later)
- Client's past event experience / what they liked or disliked
- Internal stakeholder dynamics
- Competitor intelligence

## 3. Hidden Signals

Beyond what is written, assess:

**Decision-maker type:**
- Procurement-led → emphasize process, compliance, pricing transparency, risk management
- Marketing-led → emphasize creativity, brand alignment, engagement metrics
- C-level-led → emphasize strategic impact, differentiation, headline moments

**Brief quality score** (1–5):
1. Vague — email with minimal detail
2. Basic — core facts present, many gaps
3. Standard — structured brief with clear requirements
4. Detailed — comprehensive brief with criteria and specifications
5. Thorough — full RFP with scoring, timeline, terms

**Creative temperature** (how much creative freedom):
- Cold → Execute to spec. Client knows exactly what they want.
- Warm → Guided creativity. Client has a framework but wants ideas.
- Hot → Open brief. Client is buying creative vision and innovation.

## 4. Brief Card Output

Compile the analysis into the Brief Card format (see SKILL.md). Present to the user with:

1. The filled Brief Card
2. Key gaps highlighted with proposed clarification questions
3. Recommended output type (full proposal / concept / quick response / etc.)
4. Estimated effort level for the proposal (hours, complexity)
5. Risk flags if any (tight deadline, unclear scope, budget mismatch)

## 5. Clarification Strategy

If gaps exist and timeline permits asking:

**Rule:** Ask maximum 5 questions. Bundle them. Be specific. Frame each question as helping the client get a better proposal, not as the agency being unprepared.

Example framing: "To ensure our proposal addresses your priorities precisely, we'd appreciate clarity on these points: [...]"

If no time to ask, state assumptions clearly in the proposal: "Based on the brief, we assume [X]. Our proposal can be adjusted if [Y] applies instead."
