# Dramaturgy Framework — Experience Design for Events

## Purpose

This framework provides a structured approach to designing event experiences. It draws on theatrical dramaturgy, theme park experience design, behavioral psychology, and XLAB's practice of spatial storytelling.

The core belief: **An event is a designed emotional experience.**

## 1. The Experience Arc

Every event — from a 2-hour gala to a 3-day conference — has a narrative shape. The audience may not consciously recognize it, but they feel it.

### The Five-Phase Arc

```
ANTICIPATION → ORIENTATION → IMMERSION → PEAK → RESOLUTION
```

**Phase 1: Anticipation** (before arrival)
- Begins with the first touchpoint: invitation, registration, pre-event communication
- Sets expectations, builds curiosity, establishes the visual world
- Design question: "What do attendees imagine before they arrive?"

**Phase 2: Orientation** (first 15 minutes on-site)
- The critical "first impression window" — attendees decide within minutes whether they're excited or bored
- Registration experience, welcome moment, spatial discovery
- Design question: "What is the first thing attendees see, hear, and feel when they walk in?"

**Phase 3: Immersion** (the main body)
- Attendees are in the world. Content delivery, engagement, networking, exploration.
- Must have rhythm: intensity peaks and valleys, not a flat line
- Design question: "How do we sustain attention without exhausting it?"

**Phase 4: Peak** (the memorable moment)
- Every event needs at least one moment that transcends the everyday
- This is what gets photographed, shared, remembered, and talked about
- It does not have to be the loudest moment — it can be the most intimate
- Design question: "What is the ONE moment attendees will describe to someone who wasn't there?"

**Phase 5: Resolution** (departure and aftermath)
- How the event ends shapes how it's remembered (recency bias)
- Includes: farewell moment, takeaway (physical or emotional), post-event communication
- Design question: "What feeling do attendees carry out the door?"

### Applying the Arc

For a **2-hour gala**: Each phase is 15–30 minutes. Peak is usually the main show/entertainment/reveal moment.

For a **conference day**: The arc repeats within each half-day block, with a super-arc across the full day. Morning keynote = mini-peak. Afternoon breakouts = immersion. Evening event = second arc with its own peak.

For an **installation**: The arc is spatial. Each zone IS a phase. The visitor walks through the arc.

## 2. Spatial Storytelling

Space is the medium. Every event has a geography — even a single room — and that geography tells a story.

### Zone Thinking

Divide any event space into zones with distinct purposes:

**Threshold zones:** Entry, registration, coat check. Function: transition from outside world. Design opportunity: set the tone before the "event" officially begins.

**Social zones:** Bars, lounges, networking areas. Function: connection between people. Design opportunity: create reasons for strangers to talk (interactive elements, conversation starters, shared experiences).

**Content zones:** Stages, screens, presentation areas. Function: message delivery. Design opportunity: make passive watching feel active (sight lines, proximity, participation mechanics).

**Exploration zones:** Expo, demo areas, installations. Function: self-directed discovery. Design opportunity: reward curiosity (hidden details, easter eggs, progressive reveal).

**Transition zones:** Hallways, stairs, connectors. Function: move between areas. Often ignored in design. Design opportunity: use transitions to reset, tease, or build anticipation for the next zone. (Example: Bachledka tunnel as "axon between hemispheres.")

### The Density Principle

Perceived quality correlates with density of considered detail, not with size or cost. A small, densely designed space feels more premium than a large, sparse one.

In practice: It is better to design 200m² intensely than 500m² thinly.

## 3. Engagement Architecture

### Engagement Layers

Design engagement at three concurrent levels:

**Ambient layer** (always on, requires no action)
- Music, lighting, projection loops, scent, temperature
- Creates the world even when no "content" is playing
- 80% of time, this is what the attendee experiences

**Active layer** (requires attention)
- Keynotes, performances, presentations, guided tours
- Scheduled, programmatic, directed attention
- 15% of time, concentrated impact

**Interactive layer** (requires participation)
- Touchscreens, voting, Q&A, gamification, co-creation
- Optional by nature — not everyone will engage, and that's fine
- 5% of time, but generates the strongest memories and social sharing

### The 90/10 Rule

90% of attendees will experience only the ambient and active layers. Design the event to be complete and satisfying at this level. The interactive layer is a bonus that rewards the curious.

### Modern Engagement Formats

Beyond traditional formats, consider:

- **Micro-moments:** 30-second interactions scattered through the space (a touchpoint that responds, a mirror that transforms, a floor that reacts)
- **Slow reveal:** Information or visuals that emerge over time — rewarding those who stay longer
- **Communal creation:** Attendees collectively build something (digital mural, sound composition, light sculpture) that evolves through the event
- **Digital souvenir:** Something attendees take home digitally (personalized video, AR filter, generated artwork) — extends the experience beyond the venue
- **Perspective shift:** Literally changing the physical perspective (viewing from above, from underground, in miniature) to create a memorable cognitive reframe

## 4. Technology Selection Framework

XLAB's competitive edge is technology expertise. Technology choice should always follow the experience design.

### Selection Process

```
EXPERIENCE NEED → SENSORY REQUIREMENT → TECHNOLOGY OPTION → FEASIBILITY CHECK
```

1. **What should the attendee experience?** (e.g., "feel surrounded by a living forest")
2. **What sensory input achieves this?** (e.g., "360° visual field, spatial audio, organic motion")
3. **What technology delivers this?** (e.g., "dome projection + 4.1 surround + generative content")
4. **Is it feasible?** (budget, venue, timeline, reliability, maintenance)

### Technology Palette (XLAB capabilities)

| Technology | Best for | Consideration |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| Projection mapping | Architecture, objects, large surfaces | Needs controlled lighting |
| Dome projection | Immersive environments, planetarium | Needs dedicated space |
| LED walls/screens | High-brightness, daylight, modular | Higher cost per m² |
| Holobox / hologram | Product showcase, keynote enhancement | Controlled viewing angle |
| AI Avatar / digital human | Personalized interaction, multilingual | Needs development time |
| Interactive touchscreen | Co-creation, gamification, data capture | Needs UX design |
| Spatial audio | Immersion, zone separation, narrative | Needs acoustic planning |
| Generative/real-time content | Responsive, evolving, data-driven | Needs programming |
| AR/mobile | Personal, shareable, scalable | Needs app or web platform |
| Kinetic elements | Physical wow, movement, transformation | Needs engineering + safety |

### Reliability Principle

For permanent installations and high-stakes events: every critical system has a backup path. If the projector fails, what does the audience see? If the interactive station crashes, does the experience still work?

Design the experience to degrade gracefully, not catastrophically.

## 5. Content Thinking

### Content Types in Events

**Narrative content:** Tells a story with beginning, middle, end. Used in: main shows, video packages, presentations.

**Atmospheric content:** Creates a mood without explicit narrative. Used in: ambient loops, lighting sequences, soundscapes. Often the most important content — it fills the 80% of "ambient layer" time.

**Functional content:** Communicates information. Used in: signage, schedules, wayfinding, sponsor acknowledgment. Should be beautiful AND functional.

**Interactive content:** Responds to input. Used in: touchscreens, gesture-based installations, real-time visualization.

**Generated content:** Created during the event by attendees or algorithms. Used in: social walls, co-creation installations, data-driven visualizations.

### Content Production Reality

Content production is typically the longest lead-time item and the most underestimated in proposals. Be explicit about:
- What content needs to be produced vs. sourced vs. generated
- Production timeline with approval milestones
- Client responsibilities (brand assets, footage, copy approval)
- Content update/refresh strategy for recurring or long-running installations

## 6. Measurement Framework

Every proposal should include at least a basic measurement approach. What "success" looks like depends on the event type.

### Measurement Layers

**Operational:** Did everything run on time? Were there technical failures? How many attendees?

**Engagement:** How long did people stay? Which zones were most visited? How many interacted with installations? Social media mentions?

**Perception:** Post-event survey scores. Net Promoter Score. Qualitative feedback.

**Business:** Leads generated. Media coverage achieved. Brand metric movement. Revenue (for ticketed installations).

### How to Include in Proposals

Don't over-promise measurement. But do show awareness:
- "We propose a post-event engagement report including [specific metrics]"
- "Interactive stations will capture anonymized engagement data (touches, dwell time, completion rate)"
- "We recommend a brief attendee survey focusing on [2–3 key questions]"
